# Getting Started with Science Live

Welcome to Science Live! This guide will help you start creating and discovering structured, machine-readable research knowledge in just a few minutes.

---

## What You'll Need

### A Science Live Account

Create an account at [platform.sciencelive4all.org](https://platform.sciencelive4all.org).

**To publish nanopublications**, you need an [ORCID](https://orcid.org) linked to your account. Nanopublications are signed scholarly objects — your ORCID ensures proper, persistent attribution for your contributions.

**Organizations** that want to discover, query, and commission research can create an account without an ORCID. See [For Organizations](for-organizations/overview.md).

!!! tip "ORCID is free"
    [ORCID](https://orcid.org) is free, globally recognized, and gives you a persistent digital identifier that follows you across institutions and name changes. If you don't have one yet, [register here](https://orcid.org/register) — it takes 2 minutes.

!!! tip "Browsing Without an Account"
    You can browse and search nanopublications without authentication. An account is only required to create and publish.

## Using the Web Platform

### Sign In

1. Navigate to [platform.sciencelive4all.org](https://platform.sciencelive4all.org)
2. Create an account and **link your ORCID**
3. Verify your email and complete your profile

### Create Your First Nanopublication

1. Click **Create Nanopublication** from the dashboard
2. Choose a template:
    - **CiTO (Citation)** — Describe relationships between papers
    - **AIDA (Assertion)** — Make formal research claims
    - **Quote & Comment** — Annotate specific quotes from papers
    - **Geographic Coverage** — Document study locations
    - And more...
3. Fill in the form (many fields auto-populate if you provide a paper DOI)
4. Click **Generate Nanopublication** to preview
5. Review the structured output
6. Click **Publish** to make it permanent

**Your first nanopublication is now live!** 🎉

## Understanding Nanopublications

Nanopublications are the building blocks of Science Live. Each nanopublication is a tiny, citable piece of machine-readable scientific knowledge.

### Structure

Every nanopublication contains three parts:

**Assertion** — The core claim or statement  
*Example: "Paper A supports the findings of Paper B"*

**Provenance** — Who made it, when, and how  
*Example: "Created by Jane Smith (ORCID: 0000-0002-1234-5678) on 2026-02-15"*

**Publication Info** — Metadata and licensing  
*Example: "Published under CC BY 4.0 license at https://w3id.org/np/..."*

### Benefits

- **Permanent & Citable** — Each nanopub has a persistent URI
- **Machine-Readable** — Semantic web standards (RDF/OWL)
- **Fully Traceable** — Complete provenance from claim to source
- **Globally Connected** — Part of the decentralized nanopub network

[Learn more about nanopublications](http://nanopub.net){ .md-button }

## Available Templates

Science Live provides templates for different types of research contributions:

| Template | Use Case | Example |
|----------|----------|---------|
| **CiTO Citation** | Describe paper relationships | "Paper A cites Paper B as authority" |
| **AIDA Assertion** | Make formal research claims | "Climate change affects migration patterns" |
| **Quote & Comment** | Annotate specific passages | Quote + your interpretation |
| **Geographic Coverage** | Document study locations | "Study area: Amazon rainforest (WKT coordinates)" |
| **Dataset** | Document research datasets | Dataset metadata and provenance |
| **Research Software** | Reference research software | Software used in analysis |
| **PRISMA Systematic Review** | Full systematic review workflow | PICO/PCC questions, search strategy, screening, assessment |
| **[FORRT](https://forrt.org/) Replication** | Replication studies | Original claims, study design, outcomes |

Each template generates structured, standards-compliant RDF data that integrates with the global knowledge graph.

## Common Questions

**Q: Are nanopublications really permanent?**  
A: Yes! Once published to the decentralized nanopub network, they cannot be deleted. This ensures citability. You can publish new versions if needed.

**Q: Who can see my nanopublications?**  
A: Nanopublications are public by default on the open nanopub network. This enables discovery, citation, and reuse.

**Q: Can I edit a published nanopublication?**  
A: No, but you can publish a new version that supersedes the original. The original remains for citation purposes.

**Q: Do I need to pay?**  
A: The Explorer tier is **free forever**. Advanced features unlock through quality contributions (10 nanopubs + 5 validations), not payment.

**Q: What if my paper doesn't have a DOI?**  
A: You can use any persistent URI — arXiv URLs, institutional repository links, etc.

**Q: How do I cite a nanopublication?**  
A: Each nanopublication has a persistent URI (https://w3id.org/np/...) that serves as its permanent citation.

**Q: Should I use the Web Platform or Zotero Plugin?**  
A: If you're already a Zotero user, the plugin integrates seamlessly into your workflow. Otherwise, the web platform is easier to get started with.
